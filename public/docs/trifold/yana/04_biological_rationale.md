# Biological Rationale

## The Gut–Brain Axis Framework

Diet shapes the gut microbiome, and microbial metabolites can influence neural function via the gut–brain axis. This bidirectional system includes neural, immune, endocrine, and metabolic signaling, and provides a mechanistic basis for how nutrition could affect cognition through inflammation regulation and neurotransmitter modulation.

## Fiber → Short-Chain Fatty Acids

Dietary fiber is fermented by gut bacteria into short-chain fatty acids such as acetate, propionate, and butyrate. These molecules support gut barrier integrity, regulate immune activity, and influence systemic inflammation. Because neuroinflammation can affect brain performance, SCFAs are a plausible intermediate layer linking diet composition to cognitive function.

## Tryptophan & Neuroactive Signaling

The gut microbiome also interacts with neurotransmitter-related pathways. Tryptophan is a dietary precursor for serotonin, and gut microbes can influence its availability and downstream metabolites. Certain bacterial metabolites are also associated with gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) signaling, which is relevant to inhibitory regulation and cognitive control.

## Methodological Gap

Most studies either measure diet and cognition, or microbiome and metabolites, but not all layers in the same subjects. This creates a gap between correlation and mechanism.

The teen pilot dataset includes diet and cognitive measures only. Microbiome and metabolite layers shown in this project are modeled proxies used to explore plausible mechanisms and teach how the full problem could be solved with paired biological data.
